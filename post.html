How do you deploy PoE switching?

2016-10-06 06:45 PM  

So, the question is: How do you deploy PoE Switching?

We hear from users often with questions about how to configure their PoE switches, but not often about how they are deploying PoE.

We do know that:

+More and more wireless access points, IP surveillance cameras, and VOIP deployments occur every day
+SMBs, Mom & Pop, and even consumers are finding the need for PoE switches to power these devices

But, did you know that:

+NETGEAR offers a wide range PoE and PD switches including Fully Managed, Smart Managed, Web Managed (Plus), and even Unmanaged Switches, Fast Ethernet, Gigabit Ethernet, and even Multigig Ethernet, from 5 ports to 48 ports!

+ Just within Unamanged, Web Managed, and Smart Managed switches NETGEAR has over 20 different PoE switches!

But, as always, we strive to do more and create innovative products that help our customers, so, here's your chance to influence our future (and maybe even current) products by telling us:

  1. How do you deploy PoE switching?
  2. What's your use case?
  3. What do you feel in missing from our PoE switches like the GS108PE that would help make your deployment easier?
  4. What 3 key features would you like to see in PoE switches that we currently don't offer?

RE: How do you deploy PoE switching?

2016-10-06 06:45 PM  

Hey bradleyv, I'm willing to share my experience;

  1. How do you deploy PoE switching?

    I've deployed PoE switching in a Home environment, using Business-class equipment to get the functionality I need

  2. What's your use case?

    What started as a couple of GS108Tv2's connected to a GS108PEv2 has turned into a "home-run-wiring" star-topology network with the GS108PEv2, a GS510TP, 5 x GS108Tv2's, 2 WAP's, (including the WAC720 and another vendor's 802.3af WAP), and an 802.3af Class 2 IP Phone. Purpose for PoE is twofold: 1) Don't need power plugs everywere for the network edge devices (GS108Tv2's, WAPs, IP Phone), and 2) the centrally located GS108PEv2 & GS510TP are connected to a UPS, as is the Internet modem, Firewall, and Gateway/Router - ensuring network connectivity during power outages to the household.

  3. What do you feel in missing from our PoE switches like the GS108PE that would help make your deployment easier?

    The reason why I had to buy the GS510TP was due to the GS108PEv2 being insufficient in the number of "true" PoE ports - the fact that the GS108PEv2 actually only has capacity for 3 x 802.3af Class 0 PD's (even though it has 4 PoE ports). As a customer, this is frustrating. If it's an 802.3af-compliant switch, with 4 PoE ports, it should have a power budget of 4 x 15.4 = 61.6 watts. Unfortunately, the power budget on the GS108PEv2 is 45 watts. Thus, I could not just buy a 2nd GS108PE switch, I had to move up to a GS510TP. Also, the "PoE Configuration" page from the GS510TP would be a welcome addition to the GS108PE - showing exactly how much power is being consumed (consistently across the product lines).

  4. What 3 key features would you like to see in PoE switches that we currently don't offer?

    #1 - Accurate & detailed information. For example, one PDF on the Netgear website says that the max power budget for the GS108PE is 45 watts, while another says 53 watts. Which is it? (I know it's probably the difference between v2 & v3 - but this should be spelled out in the documentation for your customers). As another example, the GS108Tv2 is difficult to plan from a power budget perspective. The datasheet says "Power consumption: 6W maximum" (so you would think it's a Class 2 PD), yet the Tech Specs says "Max consumption (Watts): 12.78 Watts if using PoE via port 1" (which would mean it's a Class 3 PD). However, given the "12V, 1.0A power adapter" supplied, the max must be 12 watts. It does not say anywhere what the PD Class is, however according to the GS510TP PoE page, a GS108Tv2 is Class 0. But the most disappointing example is the GS510TP. Turns out that its total PoE power budget is NOT 130W as stated in multiple places on the website. It's actual PoE budget is less than that - it has a threshold power of 110W.
    #2 - Missing from Netgear's lineup, and a potential market opportunity, is an 8-port PSE that falls between the GS108PE & the GS510TP. Either a Smart Managed GS508TP with 8xGbE and no SFPs (for those who don't need SFP's, and to save the extra cost of building in the SFPs), or a Web Managed Plus GS188PE with 8xGbE that are all PoE, or both!
    #3 - A PSE that offers "true to the standard" PoE capacity. For example, a GS510TP (8 x 802.3at ports) with a power budget of 240 watts.
